Mountain Rescue Team works for free for your leisure and safety

SHI MIN CHUA

At the beginning of 2026, an outpouring of donation has been made to Wasdale Mountain Rescue Team to help cover the cost of hotel fee after news of two walkers not paying the hotel that took them in after the team rescued them and arranged for them to spend the night in the hotel. However, the cost of the rescue is not limited to the hotel fee. According to the incident report, the rescue involved 8 team members who have spent their evening until after midnight (7.1 hours) to rescue the men. Of course the team did not get paid for their labour, which would be 57 hours in total, because mountain rescue teams in the UK are operated by volunteers. Besides this recent incident, back in February 2021, a headline reported a mountain rescuer fell for 150m and suffered spinal fractures while out in a mission around midnight to assist a camper with chest pain. This incident actually happened during Covid-19 lockdown when people were supposed to not mingle outside their bubbles. Despite these human cost and injuries, almost all the time, the mountain rescue teams have never been accusative of the victims for being ill-prepared or turning people off from the mountains.

In this article, I am going to visualise with some quantitative information I gathered from the incident reports of the Wasdale Mountain Rescue Team to illustrate the tough work they have put in to keep us safe in Lake District.


Mountain rescue in the UK are operated by volunteers, meaning they do not get paid for spending their time rescuing others, or assisting other civil services.


Most diverse areas tend to be in London and major cities in England, with the diversity decreasing further away from them.

Methodology

  • This visualisation is created with Python, with the interactive chropleth map generated by using Folium library while the charts in the popup and in the article by Vega-Altair.
  • The diversity score is calculated following the formula of entropy of information theory, which has been used in diversity research for ethnicity. It takes into account of the proportion of all the groups present rather than just simply using the proportion of a particular group, for example the majority group which is born in the UK in this case. The lowest score is 0, meaning that only one group of people are present, while the maximum score for 16 categories, as in the current analysis, is 2.77, when all of the groups have equal proportion.
